# Individual vs Team Metrics

Yes, with some important nuances by level. Let me work through what is viable and what breaks down at each level.

**Individual Level**

Both composites can be computed per developer, but individual-level reporting carries real risk that is worth raising explicitly before you build it.

The AI Engagement Index metrics are all available at the user level from the Copilot API — premium requests, acceptance rate, and active user status are per-seat data. The velocity composite is trickier. Cycle time and story throughput require reliable developer-to-story linkage in Rally, which your variable branching strategy may make inconsistent. Commit count per developer is straightforward from GitHub.

The bigger concern is behavioral. If individual AI metrics become visible to managers as a performance signal — even informally — you will see:
- Acceptance rate inflation (accepting suggestions uncritically to look engaged)
- Premium request padding (prompting Copilot unnecessarily to drive up counts)
- Story cherry-picking (closing easier stories to improve throughput numbers)

The research is fairly consistent on this point: **individual developer metrics should be used for coaching and self-reflection, not evaluation.** The SPACE framework authors are explicit that individual activity data is appropriate for personal dashboards and 1:1 conversations, not for ranking or performance assessment.

**Team Level**

This is where both composites are most powerful and most defensible. Aggregating to the team level smooths out individual variation, reduces gaming incentives, and produces a signal that is actually meaningful for organizational decision-making.

At the team level you can legitimately report:

```
AI Engagement Index (Team)       Velocity Composite (Team)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Avg premium requests/developer   Median cycle time
Team acceptance rate trend       Stories closed / sprint
Active user % of licensed seats  Commit count trend
```

Team-level reporting also enables something individual reporting cannot: **cross-team comparison**. Over time you can identify which teams are achieving the best correlation between AI engagement and velocity improvement — and use those teams as internal case studies for coaching and enablement. That is a direct input to your Office Hours and coaching session content.

**One structural recommendation**

Consider a three-tier reporting model rather than just individual vs. team:

- **Developer tier** — self-service only; individuals see their own data, no manager visibility; useful for personal AI fluency development
- **Team tier** — manager and team lead visibility; the primary operational reporting level; drives coaching and workflow conversations
- **Executive tier** — CIO dashboard; org-wide aggregates and trends only; the two linked composites in business terms

This structure gives you a defensible answer if anyone asks why individual data is not surfaced to leadership: it is by design, consistent with industry best practice, and protects the integrity of the team and executive metrics by reducing gaming incentives.

The one exception worth noting: if you run a formal Copilot pilot with a control group, individual-level data is appropriate for the research analysis — but that is an internal measurement exercise, not a reporting artifact.
