# Velocity Composite

That combination — Rally as the system of record, story closure as the definition of done, and variable branching — actually points to a clean answer for your third metric. Here is my thinking:

**The proposed velocity composite:**

- **Commit count** — your existing signal; useful as a relative trend indicator, not an absolute measure
- **Cycle time** — first commit on a branch to story closed in Rally; this is the most meaningful throughput signal available given your definition of done, and it spans both the coding and acceptance work
- **Story throughput rate** — stories closed per developer per sprint, derived from Rally

The third element is story throughput rate rather than something GitHub-native, and the branching variability is actually the reason why. Because branching strategy varies widely, any GitHub-side metric you derive from branch activity (like PRs, branch age, or merge frequency) will be inconsistent enough to mislead at the org level. Rally story closure, by contrast, is the one signal that means the same thing across all 350 developers regardless of how their team handles branches.

The composite tells a coherent story:

- Commit count shows *coding activity* is changing
- Cycle time shows *whether that activity is moving faster through completion*
- Story throughput shows *whether faster activity is producing more delivered value*

If all three trend upward after Copilot adoption, you have a defensible velocity narrative. If commit count rises but cycle time and throughput hold flat, that is precisely the signal that Copilot is generating motion without progress — which is the concern you had about commit count alone.

A few things worth flagging before you build this:

- You will need a reliable linkage between commits/branches and Rally stories. If your teams use story IDs in commit messages or branch names inconsistently, cycle time measurement gets noisy. It is worth assessing that data quality before committing to the composite.
- Story size normalization matters. If story points or story sizing varies significantly across teams, raw throughput counts can mislead. A team closing 10 one-point stories looks identical to a team closing 10 eight-point stories. You may want a points-weighted variant alongside raw count.
- For the CIO audience, story throughput is the most intuitive of the three — it maps directly to "are we delivering more features?" which is the question leadership actually cares about.
